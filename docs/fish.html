01 — What is Phishing

THE ART OF
DECEPTION

Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals masquerade as trusted entities — banks, coworkers, tech companies — to trick victims into revealing passwords, financial data, or clicking malware-laden links.

— CISA Definition, 2024

The word is a deliberate misspelling of "fishing" — attackers cast a wide net hoping someone bites. Modern phishing has evolved far beyond obvious spam. Today's attacks are hyper-personalised, psychologically sophisticated, and devastatingly effective even against security-savvy targets.

FROM security@paypa1.com
TO you@yourcompany.com
SUBJ ⚠ Urgent: Account Suspended

Dear Valued Customer,

We've detected unusual activity on your account. Your account access has been temporarily limited. Please verify your information immediately to restore full access.

Failure to act within 24 hours will result in permanent account closure.

Verify My Account Now

02 — Attack Vectors

KNOW YOUR
ENEMY

🎣
Phishing
Target: Everyone

Mass-distributed fraudulent emails mimicking trusted brands. Low effort, high volume. Relies on probability — even a 0.1% success rate across millions of emails is profitable.

01
🎯
Spear Phishing
Target: Specific individuals

Highly targeted attacks using personal details scraped from LinkedIn, social media, or data breaches. The attacker knows your name, role, and colleagues.

02
🐋
Whaling
Target: C-Suite executives

Spear phishing aimed at CEOs, CFOs, and board members. Attackers may impersonate lawyers, regulators, or board members. Often involves wire transfer fraud.

03
📱
Smishing
Target: Mobile users

SMS-based phishing. Fake delivery notifications, bank alerts, or prize winnings. Mobile users are more likely to click links impulsively on small screens.

04
📞
Vishing
Target: Employees & seniors

Voice phishing via phone calls. Attackers impersonate IRS agents, IT support, or bank fraud departments. AI voice cloning now makes even "familiar voices" unreliable.

05
🔍
Search Phishing
Target: Search engine users

Malicious websites paid to appear at the top of search results. You search "Chase Bank login" and click a convincing clone. Also known as SEO poisoning.

03 — Warning Signs

RED FLAGS
TO SPOT

01

Suspicious Sender Address

The display name looks right, but the actual email domain is off. "PayPal Security" sending from paypal-security@gmail.com or paypa1.com. Always check the full address.

02

Urgency & Pressure

"Act within 24 hours or your account will be closed." Panic bypasses rational thinking. Legitimate companies never create artificial emergencies to force immediate action.

03

Suspicious Links

Hover over links before clicking. The URL shown may differ from the actual destination. Watch for misspellings: "amaz0n.com", "secure-paypal.net", or obscure domains.

04

Unexpected Attachments

Invoices, shipping notices, or documents you weren't expecting. Even seemingly safe file types (.pdf, .docx) can contain malicious macros or embedded exploit code.

05

Generic Greetings

"Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name suggests a mass-sent attack. Your real bank, employer, or service provider knows who you are.

06

Requests for Sensitive Data

No legitimate company will ever ask for your password, full SSN, or credit card number via email. Ever. This is an absolute rule, with no exceptions.

07

Too Good to Be True

You won a raffle you never entered. An inheritance from a distant relative. A surprise tax refund. If an email brings unexpected good news requiring action — it's bait.

08

Poor Grammar & Spelling

Typos, awkward phrasing, or unusual capitalization can signal a non-native attacker or automated generation. Though AI has made polished phishing far more common.

04 — Defense Strategies

HOW TO
PROTECT YOURSELF

🔐

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication

MFA is your most powerful single defense. Even if attackers steal your password, they cannot access your account without the second factor — your phone, hardware key, or authenticator app.

→ Use an authenticator app (Authy, Google Authenticator) over SMS when possible. SIM-swap attacks can intercept SMS codes.
🔑

Use a Password Manager

Password managers auto-fill credentials only on the correct domain. If you're on a phishing clone, it won't fill in — an instant red flag. They also generate unique passwords per site.

→ Recommended: Bitwarden (free, open-source), 1Password, or Dashlane.
🧠

Verify Before You Act

Got a suspicious email from your bank or IT department? Don't click anything. Go directly to the website by typing the URL yourself, or call the number on the back of your card.

→ For workplace requests, call the person directly using a phone number you already have — not one provided in the email.
📧

Use Email Filtering Tools

Enable spam filters and consider third-party email security tools. Look for services that scan attachments in sandboxes, validate sender domains via DMARC/SPF/DKIM, and flag anomalies.

→ For businesses: Microsoft Defender for Office 365, Proofpoint, or Mimecast.
🔄

Keep Software Updated

Phishing often delivers malware exploiting known software vulnerabilities. Keeping your OS, browser, and apps updated patches these holes before attackers can use them.

→ Enable auto-updates wherever possible. Unpatched systems are low-hanging fruit for attackers.
📚

Train Yourself Regularly

Phishing tactics evolve constantly. Annual security training is no longer enough. Subscribe to security newsletters, take simulated phishing tests, and stay current on new attack patterns.

→ Free resources: Google's Phishing Quiz, KnowBe4 free tools, CISA security guides.
